Motivating & Tracking Reading in Children

By Michelle Wong Yu Miin

Problem Description

How do we track reading progress in children and motivate them to read?

Encouraging reading in children is important as research suggests that early-reading helps in the development of reading-related cognitive skills. (Kalb, G. & van Ours, J. C., 2013) As such, parents have been looking for ways to encourage, foster and monitor their childrenâ€™s reading habits. (Quora, n.d.) Currently there are apps available that track reading progress in e-books, such as Kindle and ReadEra. Apps that teach children to pronounce words and learn to construct sentences, like Starfall and Homer, are also available as applications and websites.

The studies for early reading in children primarily focuses on parents reading to their children, (Niklas, F., Cohrssen, C., & Tayler, C., 2016) but parents may not recall how many books they have read to their child. The applications I mentioned primarily concern reading material available within the applications or e-books. However, I have not found apps that provide additional support for physical children's books. I feel that an application that allows both physical and digital books to be tracked and generate checkpoints to achieve goals would provide a more comprehensive report on a child's reading progress and facilitate additional avenues of incentive. For the purposes of this project, I will be focusing on English-speaking children in the United States.

Proposed Application

The main users of this application will be parents of children between the ages of 3 to 12 years old. These parents will be inputting their children's reading progress in the form of adding books and noting the pages they have gone through. This is to ensure that children are unable to cheat the system.

The statistics that are shown will appear in the form of line graphs and bar charts showing the number of pages and books read over days, months, and years. The number of hours spent reading can also be recorded. As more books are read, a virtual plant is grown on the site and children can try to make the plant produce more flowers by reading more.

In this way, I hope children will be motivated to get the tallest plant among their friends. Parents can add their children's friends and in this way, foster a competitive spirit and add additional motivation.

User Journeys

Completing a book

  1. The parent signs up for an account on the app for one of their children.
  2. The parent reads through a book with their child for bedtime.
  3. They record the name of the book and how many pages were covered to complete the book.
  4. A plant is created and a flower blooms for the completed book.

Finishing a book halfway

  1. The parent has an existing account with their child.
  2. The parent reads halfway through a book with their child for bedtime.
  3. They record the name of the book and how many pages were covered as well as the number of pages needed to complete the book.
  4. A plant is created and the flowerbud appears on the plant for the book in-progress.

Comparing progress to other parents

  1. Another parent signs up for an account on the app for their children.
  2. This parent adds their child's friend code as they have also signed up.
  3. They can now view the other child's plant.
  4. Their child can compete with others to grow a taller plant.
